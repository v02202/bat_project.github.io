Climate change

Climate change has increasingly threatened ecosystems. Changes in air and land surface temperatures, extreme climate events, such as droughts and floods, and climate variability are anticipated to affect different aspects of biodiversity, from species to community levels (Bellard et al. 2012; Bhattacharjee et al. 2017; IPCC 2019). Climate variability represents the meteorological condition that differs from the mean condition (Porter and Semenov 2005) in terms of temperature, precipitation, or the interaction of the two. Climate variability has become more intense in recent years through different climate variables and temporal scales. For example, changes in precipitation variability may elevate the endangerment of heat and drought sensitive species in cloud forests (Anderson et al. 2011). In addition to the impact on the species level, communities could also be affected through changes in species interactions. For instance, temperature variability may disrupt food chains due to species’ different thermal tolerance, and thus results in a mismatch of inter-specific relationships (Woodward et al. 2016). Therefore, as climate variability is projected to change under climate change (IPCC 2001, 2019), understanding the mechanism how climate variability at different temporal scales influence community structure is essential for policymakers to improve the management to avoid the negative impact of climate change on human life.

Community assebly

The process that species from the regional area build a local community is called community assembly, which is determined by species’ dispersal ability, their responses to the abiotic environment and their interactions with other species (HilleRisLambers et al. 2012; Kraft and Ackerly 2013). In this process, mechanisms undergo with the species’ functional traits, which present the response of species or individual to the environment and the ecological functions provided by (Díaz and Cabido 2001).
Dispersal
    Dispersal ability determines the potential members in a community, since only the species who can arrive the location have the opportunity to be a member of that community (Kraft and Ackerly 2013).
Environment
    Environmental factors are known as the environmental filter, preventing those species which cannot tolerate the environment at the location from establishing a viable population. This is one of the most enduring concepts in the studies of community assembly and is always related to the traits of species because traits are measured at the individual level and compared across species (McGill et al. 2006).
The tolerance curve of individuals or higher taxonomic groupings defined by an environmental optimum could interpret the probable trait range (Lynch & Gabriel 1987; Sexton et al. 2017). That is, traits could play an intermediate role between species and environmental stress for representing a convergence in ecological function (van der Valk 1981; Keddy 1992; Kraft et al. 2015). For example, in a species‐rich tropical system, the plants whose leaves have traits associated with high thermal tolerance dominate the hot and dry early- successional sites, indicating the influence of environmental filtering on the community structure (Edwin et al. 2010). The general consensus that functional diversity attributes to community process has been growing (Grime 1997; Chapin et al. 2000). Functional diversity expresses the value and distribution of species traits in an ecosystem, thus reflecting many aspects of ecological functions provided by the species or individual (Dı´az and Cabido 2001, Tilman 2001, Petchey and Gaston 2006). For example, environmental filter is tested in California woody-plant community based on their traits, and the result indicated that the range of traits is less than expected from random assembly because of the environmental filter (Cornwell et al. 2006).
Competition
    Finally, biotic factors, like competition, may further affect the species composition of a community. Trait value within the range of a community is influenced by competition, species with similar traits may have a more intense competition because of the limited resource (MacArthur and Levins 1967).
Multiple functional traits are measured to investigate the species performance in interspecific competition because phytoplankton in freshwater marine have different functional constraints on using nitrate and phosphate (Edwards, Klausmeier and Litchman 2011). Addition to the strong link between ecological process, functional diversity is usually measured by multiple traits to describe different functions, so functional diversity is used to understand the subject cannot answered by species diversity (Dı´az and Cabido 2001; McGill et al. 2006, HilleRisLambers et al. 2012). It is important to understand the underlying mechanism influencing functional diversity under temperature variability, yet the current studies is limited and much of them only focus on the relationship of rising mean temperature and species diversity.
